How it works

No paid data, no manual input. Every step below runs unattended on a schedule.

01

Pull the odds

Live moneylines fetched every morning for every MLB game happening that day, filtered to games that haven't started yet.

02

Pull the stats

Season record, last-10 form, home/away splits, starting pitcher ERA, bullpen ERA, team offense, and platoon splits vs the probable starter's handedness, all from MLB's free public API.

03

Score both teams

Six weighted signals combine into a single win-probability estimate for each side of the matchup.

04

Check the price

Win probability is compared against the market price. Outlier bookmaker quotes and unrealistic edges are automatically discarded.

05

Write the reasoning

Every qualifying pick gets a plain-English explanation, built from whichever real factors favored it.

06

Log, post, grade

Picks post automatically, then get checked against final scores the next day to build a running accuracy record.

Inside the model

The confidence score isn't a black box. Here's the actual scoring function.

main.py
def confidence_score(fair_prob, edge, book_spread, num_books):
    """0-100 confidence rating that leads with win probability, not edge
    size. EV and book agreement matter, but as secondary boosts.
    - win probability itself: up to 60 pts (the main driver)
    - positive edge, capped: up to 20 pts
    - agreement across books: up to 15 pts
    - how many books we're averaging: up to 5 pts
    """
    prob_score = fair_prob * 60
    edge_score = min(max(edge, 0) / 0.15, 1.0) * 20
    agreement_score = max(0, 1 - (book_spread / 0.50)) * 15
    volume_score = min(num_books / 5, 1.0) * 5
    return round(prob_score + edge_score + agreement_score + volume_score)

Win probability is weighted 60% of the score by design, confidence tracks how likely a team is to win first, market edge second.

Built-in safeguards

A model is only as good as its guardrails. A few things it refuses to do:

No small-sample trust

A pitcher with under 15 career innings gets treated as unknown, not judged off a lucky start.

No stale prices

Bookmaker quotes that are wildly out of line with the rest get dropped before averaging.

No implausible edges

An edge over 30% almost always means bad data, not a real opportunity, so it's discarded automatically.

No already-started games

Games that have kicked off are filtered out before the model ever looks at them.
